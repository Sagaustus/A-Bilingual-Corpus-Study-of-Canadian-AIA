Figure 1
Comparative Term Frequencies (EN vs FR)
Figure 1. Side-by-side comparison of 15 governance terms across English (blue) and French (orange) AI Impact Assessments. N = 751 total occurrences, 16 bilingual pairs, 2019–2024.
Key Insight: Only 3 terms show perfect parity (transparency, discrimination, explanation). The majority exhibit moderate-to-severe divergence, with monitoring/contrôle showing 5.4× frequency difference.

Interpretation

This visualization reveals three tiers of semantic alignment: (1) Perfect equivalence for internationally standardized terms like "discrimination"; (2) Near-parity for fairness concepts; (3) Severe fragmentation for governance oversight terms (accountability, monitoring, audit, review).

Figure 2
Semantic Divergence Analysis
Figure 2. Ratio of French-to-English term frequencies, showing degree of semantic alignment. Ratios near 1.0 (green zone) indicate parity; ratios >2.0 or <0.5 (red zones) indicate severe divergence.
Key Insight: 7 out of 15 terms fall outside the parity zone (0.8–1.2), indicating systematic rather than isolated divergence. Contrôle shows 5.4× over-representation vs monitoring.

Interpretation

The divergence pattern is not random. High-divergence terms cluster in governance oversight domains (monitoring → contrôle, review → évaluation, audit → vérification), suggesting different administrative philosophies: EN emphasizes passive observation, FR emphasizes active control and verification.

Governance Category Comparison
Figure 3. Grouped comparison of governance terms by semantic category: Oversight, Verification, Fairness, and Transparency. Reveals category-level divergence patterns.
Key Insight: Oversight category shows most severe divergence (monitoring 5× vs contrôle 27×). Fairness and transparency categories approach parity, suggesting shared conceptual frameworks in rights-based discourse.

Interpretation

Category-level analysis reveals that divergence is domain-specific. Rights-based terminology (fairness, discrimination, transparency) shows convergence, likely influenced by international human rights frameworks. Administrative terminology (oversight, verification) shows divergence reflecting distinct governance traditions.

Figure 5
⭐ SPOTLIGHT FINDING
The Accountability Gap
Figure 5. The accountability gap: English uses unified term "accountability" (15×) while French distributes across reddition de comptes (11×), imputabilité (4×), and responsabilité (1×). Total FR = 16, exceeding EN due to conceptual fragmentation.
🔴 Critical Finding: The term "accountability" appears ZERO times as direct equivalent in French AIAs. Instead, French employs three distinct terms reflecting different juridical and administrative traditions: legal responsibility (responsabilité), administrative reporting (reddition de comptes), and technical attribution (imputabilité).

Interpretation

This is the paper's central finding. The accountability gap exemplifies how official bilingualism produces parallel meaning systems. Where English treats accountability as a superordinate governance concept, French fragments it into domain-specific subordinates:

  • Responsabilité (1×) — Legal liability under Quebec civil law
  • Reddition de comptes (11×) — Administrative reporting under federal accountability frameworks
  • Imputabilité (4×) — Technical attribution in systems engineering

This is not translation error — it's conceptual divergence rooted in distinct juridical traditions.
