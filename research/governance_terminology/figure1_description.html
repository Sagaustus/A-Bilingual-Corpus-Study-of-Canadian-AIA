Figure 1
Comparative Term Frequencies: EN vs FR
Figure 1. Comparative frequencies of 15 governance terms across 16 bilingual AI Impact Assessment pairs. Blue bars represent English terms, orange bars represent French terms (or combined French equivalents). N = 751 total term occurrences, 2019–2024.

Key Findings

  • Assessment/Évaluation: Highest frequency pair (EN: 290, FR: 313) — French slightly higher, reflecting broader semantic scope of "évaluation"
  • Audit/Vérification: Divergent usage (EN: 121, FR: 9 for "audit" / FR: 167 for "vérification") — French prefers broader verification terminology
  • Review/Révision: Severe divergence (EN: 119, FR: 3) — French uses "évaluation" instead
  • Accountability: EN uses single term (15×) while FR distributes across responsabilité (1), reddition de comptes (11), imputabilité (4)
  • Monitoring vs Contrôle: EN monitoring (5×) vs FR contrôle (27×) — 5.4× difference reveals control-centric governance philosophy

Interpretation

This comparative visualization reveals three patterns of semantic alignment:

1. Perfect Parity (Green zone): Terms like transparency/transparence, discrimination, and explanation/explication show 1:1 correspondence, suggesting shared conceptual frameworks in these domains.

2. Moderate Divergence (Yellow zone): Terms like fairness/équité and recourse/recours show near-parity with minor variance (0.8–1.2 ratio), indicating functional equivalence despite different usage frequencies.

3. Severe Divergence (Red zone): The most significant finding — terms like accountability, monitoring, audit, and review show fragmentation in French, where single English concepts map to multiple French terms with distinct semantic fields.

"Where English documents employ unified governance terms, French versions distribute these concepts across multiple lexemes reflecting distinct administrative, legal, and technical traditions. This is not translation variance — it is conceptual divergence embedded in bilingual policy infrastructure."

For CSDH 2026 Paper

Research Question: How does official bilingualism produce parallel meaning systems in algorithmic governance?

This Figure Shows: Systematic semantic drift rather than isolated translation errors. The clustering of high-divergence terms (accountability, monitoring, audit) in governance oversight domains suggests different conceptual frameworks for algorithmic regulation.

Theoretical Contribution: Challenges assumptions about policy coherence in bilingual jurisdictions. Official bilingualism may inadvertently produce divergent governance epistemologies.

Methodology: Computational term extraction from 32 PDF documents (16 EN, 16 FR) using pdfminer.six. Term frequencies calculated with ±50 character context windows. Visualization created with matplotlib 3.8.0, 300 DPI PNG export.
Source: Canadian federal AI Impact Assessments (TBS, IRCC, VAC, ESDC, Transport, PHAC, CBSA)
Analysis Date: January 2026
