# CSDH 2026 Conference Submission

## Title
**The Semantics of Accountability: Meaning Drift in Bilingual AI Impact Assessments**

## Abstract (250 words)

Canada's commitment to official bilingualism assumes semantic equivalence across English and French policy documents. Yet how do meaning systems diverge when governance frameworks are translated? This paper examines semantic drift in 16 bilingual AI Impact Assessments (AIAs) produced by Canadian federal institutions between 2019-2024, revealing systematic divergence in how algorithmic accountability is conceptualized across languages.

Using computational text analysis combined with large language model-assisted semantic matching, we extracted and analyzed 751 governance term occurrences from 32 parallel documents. Our methodology first identified bilingual document pairs through GPT-4-based semantic matching—revealing that 38% of federal AIAs exist bilingually despite appearing monolingual through traditional cataloging. We then performed frequency analysis and contextual divergence detection across 20+ high-frequency governance terms.

Our findings reveal a striking "accountability gap": the English term *accountability* appears 15 times across documents while its presumed French equivalent *responsabilité* appears zero times in corresponding contexts. Instead, French documents employ *reddition de comptes* (11 occurrences) and *imputabilité* (4 occurrences)—terms embedding different juridical and administrative traditions. Similarly, *monitoring* (EN: 5) diverges from *contrôle* (FR: 27), reflecting distinct governance philosophies rather than simple translation.

This semantic drift has material consequences: risk assessment frameworks, stakeholder roles, and mitigation strategies shift meaning across language versions of identical systems. Our computational approach—combining term extraction, collocation analysis, and LLM-based divergence detection—offers a scalable methodology for identifying conceptual slippage in bilingual governance. We argue that Canada's algorithmic governance infrastructure inadvertently produces parallel meaning systems, challenging assumptions about policy coherence in officially bilingual states.

**Keywords:** algorithmic governance, official bilingualism, computational text analysis, semantic drift, translation studies
